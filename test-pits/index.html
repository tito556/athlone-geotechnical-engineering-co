The most costly mistake on Athlone sites isn't usually structural failure — it's assuming the ground is uniform. The town straddles the River Shannon, and decades of urban development have buried old channels, backfilled quays, and industrial deposits beneath a thin skin of modern pavement. We have seen projects stall for weeks because a contractor hit saturated alluvium at two metres that desk studies never flagged. An exploratory test pit cuts through the guesswork. By excavating to depths of 3.5 to 4.5 metres and logging the sequence in-situ, you see the real layering — organic silts, river gravels, or weathered Carboniferous limestone — exactly where your footing will sit. For deeper bearing verification beyond the pit base, we often pair this investigation with a targeted SPT drilling campaign to prove refusal depth on bedrock.

A single test pit can reveal more about lateral soil variability than three boreholes spaced twenty metres apart.

Service characteristics in Athlone

IS EN 1997-2:2007 (Eurocode 7 – Ground investigation) requires that the investigation scope matches the geotechnical category of the project. In Athlone, where the Shannon floodplain deposits transition sharply into glacial till and limestone pinnacles within a few hundred metres, relying solely on borehole data can miss lateral variability. An exploratory test pit exposes a continuous vertical face that reveals bedding, fissuring, and perched water tables that rotary drilling can smear or bypass. During logging, we apply BS 5930:2015 description conventions — recording consistency, colour, inclusions, and moisture condition — and photograph each face with scale and orientation. Where the project involves roads or car parks, the exposed subgrade can be sampled directly for laboratory CBR testing to validate design assumptions against the actual material, not a generic classification.

Risks and considerations in Athlone

The Shannon alluvium beneath Athlone's commercial centre contains lenses of soft organic clay that oxidise within hours upon exposure. If these layers go unidentified, differential settlement can crack masonry walls and rack steel frames before the building is even signed off. In the western suburbs, where glacial till overlies karstic limestone, the risk shifts to pinnacle-and-socket weathering — a footing bearing on a thin rock bridge over a clay-filled fissure may punch through under load. An exploratory test pit lets you trace the rockhead profile across the footprint and decide whether you need to deepen the excavation, switch to a raft, or specify stone columns to bridge soft pockets. Without this information, you are designing for a ground model that may not exist, and the remediation bill after discovery is always a multiple of the investigation cost.

Our services

Every pit is logged by an experienced engineering geologist who understands Athlone's glacial and fluvial history — not just a machine operator ticking boxes. The following services are included as standard in our test pit investigations.

Geotechnical logging and face mapping

Detailed stratigraphic log to BS 5930:2015 with scaled photographs, discontinuity spacing, and groundwater strike depth. Each stratum is described for colour, plasticity, density, and inclusions.

Sampling and in-situ testing

Recovery of disturbed and undisturbed samples for laboratory classification; hand shear vane tests on cohesive soils; infiltration testing for SUDS design where required by planning.

Backfill and reinstatement specification

Written procedure for engineered backfill using excavated arisings or imported granular fill, with compaction validation by nuclear densometer or sand replacement. We specify lift thickness and moisture conditioning ranges.

Quick answers

What is the typical cost of an exploratory test pit in Athlone?

For a standard machine-excavated pit to 3.5–4.0 metres depth with full geotechnical logging, photography, sampling, and a factual report, the cost typically falls between €520 and €790 per pit. The final figure depends on access constraints, the number of pits, and whether traffic management or confined-space precautions are required.

Do I need a test pit, or would a borehole be better?

Each method answers different questions. A test pit lets you see and feel the soil structure, measure joint spacing in rock, and take large undisturbed block samples. A borehole reaches greater depth and allows SPTs. On Athlone sites with suspected shallow made ground or variable rockhead, we usually recommend at least two pits to calibrate the borehole logs.

How close to existing foundations can you excavate?

Safety is the governing factor. Under the Construction Regulations, we maintain a setback of at least 1.0 metre from shallow footings unless a temporary works design is prepared. We assess the zone of influence based on soil type and depth — in Athlone's soft alluvium, that zone can extend further than in the stiffer glacial tills.

What happens if groundwater enters the pit?

We record the strike depth and measure the inflow rate. If the pit is stable and water inflow is modest, logging continues with notes on seepage pathways. For high inflows, we may need to pump and observe the recovery rate, which provides a direct measurement of mass permeability far more representative than a single falling-head test in a borehole.

How quickly do you deliver the report after the fieldwork?

A factual report with logs, photographs, and laboratory test schedules is typically issued within five to seven working days of completing fieldwork. If you need preliminary feedback to keep a concrete pour on schedule, we can provide verbal bullet points and a draft log on the same day as the excavation.
