A set of 200 mm diameter brass sieves stacked on a Ro-Tap shaker forms the mechanical backbone of any grain size analysis in Athlone. The sieve stack runs from 75 mm down to 75 microns, capturing the coarse fraction while the minus-75-micron material washes through into a sedimentation cylinder. For the fine fraction a 152H hydrometer, pre-calibrated at 20 °C, settles into a 1000 ml graduated cylinder filled with a sodium hexametaphosphate dispersion. The hydrometer stem readings at 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 15, 30, 60, 120, 240 and 1440 minutes build the sedimentation curve via Stokes' law. Between the coarse sieve data and the hydrometer fines the lab produces a continuous particle size distribution curve from gravel to clay colloids. When borehole samples arrive from the Shannon callows or from glacial till exposures east of town the combined ASTM D422 procedure resolves the full grading envelope in about 48 hours.

A full sieve-plus-hydrometer curve on Athlone lake silts often reveals a clay fraction below 2 microns that governs permeability and frost heave risk.

Service characteristics in Athlone

The Quaternary geology beneath Athlone is dominated by two contrasting formations: the Shannon gravels — fluvioglacial outwash with rounded limestone clasts up to 150 mm — and the thick lacustrine silts of the former proglacial lake that occupied the basin during deglaciation. In our experience the gravel fraction often exceeds 40 percent by mass, which forces a larger initial sample mass and careful splitting to avoid segregation bias. The silt beds by contrast yield over 60 percent passing the 75-micron sieve, pushing the analysis firmly into hydrometer territory. What we see most often in road scheme investigations along the N6 bypass corridor is a gap-graded fabric where gravel and silt dominate but the sand fraction is scarce — a gradation that complicates compaction and drainage. For projects where the grading directly feeds pavement design we pair the grain size curve with a CBR test for road construction to link particle distribution to bearing capacity. When the silt content exceeds 35 percent and plasticity is suspected the lab adds Atterberg limits testing on the same sample split, because grading alone cannot predict the behaviour of the Athlone lake silts under repeated wetting.

Risks and considerations in Athlone

The most common mistake we see on Athlone sites is relying on a sieve-only analysis when the sample contains more than about 10 percent fines. A sieve stack cannot resolve the silt and clay fractions, and engineers end up with a D10 value that is completely fictitious — which feeds directly into permeability estimates, filter design and frost susceptibility assessments that fail in service. On one flood relief scheme west of the town centre a contractor placed filter sand specified from a sieve-only curve only to find the drainage blanket clogged within two winters because the actual fines content was 28 percent. The hydrometer leg of the ASTM D422 procedure would have shown the clay tail below 2 microns in the first 24 hours. Another risk is under-sampling: taking a 500 g bag from a gravelly till and expecting it to represent the full grading. For Athlone's fluvioglacial deposits with cobbles up to 150 mm the minimum field sample mass should be 25 kg, reduced by quartering in the lab.

Our services

The grain size analysis is rarely ordered in isolation. On Athlone projects the grading curve typically feeds several parallel workstreams, and the same sample can serve multiple test sequences if split correctly at the start.

Combined sieve and hydrometer (full curve)

Mechanical sieving from 75 mm to 75 µm plus 24-hour hydrometer sedimentation. Delivers the complete particle size distribution, coefficient of uniformity Cu, coefficient of curvature Cc, and percentage gravel, sand, silt and clay fractions. Suitable for classification to the USCS or BS system.

Wash-sieve analysis only (coarse soils)

For clean gravels and sands where the fines content is visibly below 5 percent. Sample washed over a 75 µm sieve, dried, and dry-sieved. Faster turnaround and lower cost than the full combined method.

Hydrometer-only analysis (fine soils)

Applied to silt and clay samples where the entire material passes the 2 mm sieve. Sedimentation readings over 24 hours with temperature correction and meniscus correction. Reports clay fraction below 2 µm.

Grading for pavement and filter design

Targeted reporting of D10, D15, D30, D50, D60 and D85 with grading envelopes plotted against TII/NRA Series 600 or Series 500 specification bands. Used for filter compatibility checks and asphalt/base course aggregate compliance on Athlone road schemes.

Quick answers

How much does a combined sieve and hydrometer grain size analysis cost in Athlone?

The combined ASTM D422 test — mechanical sieving plus 24-hour hydrometer sedimentation — typically runs between €110 and €160 per sample. The exact price depends on whether the sample needs pre-treatment for organic content, the maximum particle size (which dictates the initial mass), and the number of reporting parameters required. Samples with cobbles above 75 mm that require field sieving before the lab split add a small surcharge.

What sample mass do I need to send to the lab for a reliable grain size analysis?

It depends entirely on the maximum particle size visible in the field. For Athlone's Shannon gravels with cobbles up to 150 mm we recommend at least 25 kg of representative material, sealed in a heavy-duty bag to prevent moisture loss. For the fine lacustrine silts a 1 kg bag is sufficient. The key is representativeness — grab samples from a single spot in a heterogeneous glacial till will mislead the entire grading curve.

How long does the hydrometer sedimentation phase take?

The standard ASTM D422 hydrometer procedure requires readings at 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 15, 30, 60, 120, 240 and 1440 minutes after mixing. That means the sedimentation leg alone spans 24 hours. Together with sieve drying, washing and data reduction the full combined analysis is typically reported within 48 hours of sample receipt. Rush turnaround can be arranged for urgent Athlone projects.

Why can't I just do a sieve analysis and skip the hydrometer?

A sieve stack cannot resolve particles below 75 microns — the silt and clay fractions simply pass through the finest sieve and are recorded as a single 'pan' mass. You lose all information about the grading below that point. For Athlone's lake silts, where over 60 percent of the material can be sub-75-micron, a sieve-only result gives no D10, no clay fraction, and no basis for permeability or frost heave assessment.
